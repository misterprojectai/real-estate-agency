# 03 Client Communication — Examples

These examples show how voice profiles and team standards work together in a draft — not just what the message says, but the reasoning that produces it. A new agent reading these hears Diana thinking out loud about each situation. The output is complete in every case: ready to send without modification.

---

## Example 1: competing-offer — buyer at budget ceiling, Austin seller's market

### Situation
Maria and David are first-time buyers under contract on a Hyde Park bungalow at $580k — their ceiling. A competing offer arrived this morning. Their agent Sara needs to communicate their position. The stakes: if Sara manufactures urgency to push them to waive contingencies they need, and the deal falls through or they overpay, Diana's team loses a client relationship and a referral network permanently.

### Context loaded
**From Lead Card (`lead_id: LEAD-20250512-SLR`):**
- budget_max: $580,000 — they are already at ceiling. No room to escalate on price.
- pre_approved: yes — they have financing. Their position is real.
- urgency: 4 — motivated, not desperate. They will walk if this doesn't feel right.
- must_haves: single-story, no HOA, walkable to coffee — Hyde Park fits.
- dealbreakers: foundation issues, flood zone — not present on this property.

**Voice profile:** sara.md
- Direct and warm. Doesn't hedge or over-qualify. Clients trust her because she tells them the truth, not what they want to hear.

**From `_config/team-standards.md`:**
- Section loaded: Hard moments playbook — Competing offers
- "Assess the client's true urgency and their actual financial ceiling before advising any escalation. Never manufacture urgency. Give clients clarity about their position and options — then let them decide."

### Diana's reasoning
Maria and David are at their ceiling. A less experienced agent might push them to escalate anyway — "just go $5k over" — because closing the deal feels like success. But $580k is their ceiling for a reason. The playbook says give clarity, not pressure. Sara's job here is to lay out their actual position honestly: they are competitive, they are pre-approved, they cannot escalate on price, and there are things they can offer besides price (quick close, flexible possession, clean offer). Then let them decide. If they lose the house, they lose it with full information. That is what builds a relationship that sends three referrals, not a transaction that closes once.

### Output
Hi Maria and David —

Wanted to give you a quick update: there is a competing offer on the Hyde Park house. Here is where you stand.

Your offer is strong. You are pre-approved, your offer is clean, and you are at $580,000 — which is a serious number for this property. You are not in a weak position.

What you cannot do is escalate further on price — we have been clear about your ceiling and I want to honor that, not talk you into something that does not work for your finances.

What you can do: tighten your timeline if the sellers need a fast close, offer flexible possession if they need more time to move, or write a brief personal note to the sellers. These things matter to some sellers more than a few thousand dollars.

I want you to make the decision that is right for you, not the one that feels urgent in the moment. If this house is the one, we go in with everything we have. If it is not, there will be another — and you will be in the same strong position you are in right now.

Let me know how you want to proceed. I can move quickly once you decide.

— Sara

*Tone notes: Direct, warm, no pressure. Sara's voice — tells clients the truth, trusts them to decide.*
*Recommended send time: Within 30 minutes of learning about the competing offer.*
*Follow-up trigger: If no response in 2 hours, call — do not send another message.*

### Why this meets team standards
Honors the non-negotiable "never manufactures urgency for the agent's benefit" — the draft gives Maria and David options and explicitly protects them from being pressured into a decision that exceeds their ceiling. Applies "we are advocates, not salespeople" — Sara is giving them information to make their own decision, not closing language designed to push them forward. This draft would pass Diana's review because it protects the client relationship over the transaction; it would fail a generic real estate review because it does not try to close.

## Example 2: inspection-response — Travis Heights bungalow, foundation concern in inspection report

### Situation
Marcus is representing buyers on a Travis Heights bungalow. The inspection came back with a foundation crack flagged as "monitor, may indicate active movement" — not a deal-killer per the inspector, but worth addressing. The sellers are motivated but have not offered any concession. Marcus needs to communicate with his clients — James and Karen — about what this means and what their options are. James and Karen are experienced buyers (urgency 3, second home purchase) with foundation issues explicitly listed as a dealbreaker on their Lead Card.

### Context loaded
**From Lead Card (`lead_id: LEAD-20250428-MRK`):**
- urgency: 3 — not desperate, not browsing
- dealbreakers: foundation issues — this inspection finding hits their stated dealbreaker directly
- must_haves: single-story, mature trees, established neighborhood
- pre_approved: yes / budget_max: $695,000 / property at $649,000

**Voice profile:** marcus.md
- Measured and thorough. Clients trust him to know the details. He does not rush to reassure — he explains what is actually known and what is not.

**From `_config/team-standards.md`:**
- Section loaded: Hard moments playbook — Inspection issues
- "Separate structural from cosmetic. Know which repairs to fight for based on the client's stated must-haves and dealbreakers — not based on the agent's desire to close. Frame every finding as 'here is what this means for you and here are your options.'"
- "The agent's job is to inform, not to push. A client who makes an informed decision they later regret trusts us more than one who felt pressured into the wrong one."

### Diana's reasoning
Foundation issues are James and Karen's stated dealbreaker. A less experienced agent might soften the finding — "the inspector said it's probably fine, just needs monitoring." That framing is a betrayal. The inspector flagged it. Diana's team does not smooth over what clients said they cannot accept. Marcus's job is to give James and Karen exactly what the inspection says, explain what the finding means in plain terms, and lay out their actual options: negotiate a concession, get a structural engineer assessment before proceeding, or walk during the option period. The client decides. Marcus does not choose for them.

### Output
Hi James and Karen —

Got the full inspection report back. Overall the house is in solid shape for its age — but there is one finding I want to walk you through before you decide how to proceed.

The inspector flagged a crack in the front foundation pier. His language: "monitor, may indicate active movement." That is inspector-speak for: it could be settling (common and manageable) or it could indicate active foundation movement (a bigger problem). He cannot tell from a visual inspection alone.

Here is what this means for you:

**Option 1 — Negotiate a concession.** We go back to the sellers and ask for a credit or repair based on the finding. They are motivated sellers, so there is likely room here. The risk: a credit does not tell you the scope of what you are actually dealing with.

**Option 2 — Get a structural engineer out before the option period ends.** This is what I would recommend. A structural engineer can assess whether the crack is historic settling or active movement — that assessment changes everything about how you think about this property. Cost is typically $350–500 and can be done within 2–3 days. If it comes back clean, you proceed with confidence. If it does not, you have the information you need to make the right call.

**Option 3 — Walk.** You listed foundation issues as a dealbreaker. That is a legitimate reason to terminate during the option period. This is a choice you can make, and it does not have to be framed as losing — it is the option period doing exactly what it is there for.

I can reach out to the sellers' agent today about either a credit request or a brief extension to get the engineer in. Tell me which direction you want to go.

— Marcus

*Tone notes: Thorough and measured. Three options laid out plainly — no steering, no softening. Foundation issue named directly.*
*Recommended send time: Same day as inspection delivery.*
*Follow-up trigger: Call if no response by end of business — this decision has an option period deadline.*

### Why this meets team standards
Names the dealbreaker directly rather than softening it. Presents three options without steering toward any of them — "we are advocates, not salespeople." The structural engineer recommendation is Marcus's professional judgment, not pressure: it gives James and Karen the information they need to make the right decision. "Hard conversations happen early, when they can still change outcomes" — and this message does exactly that. A generic inspection response would say "the inspector flagged a minor foundation issue, very common, nothing to worry about." This one does not.

## Example 3: financing-delay — Mueller condo, day 20, lender has gone quiet

### Situation
Priya is representing buyers on a Mueller condo at $549,000. The financing deadline is day 30. It is day 20 and the lender, who was responsive through underwriting, has gone quiet since the appraisal was submitted four days ago. Priya does not know if there is a problem or if the lender is just slow. The buyers — Carlos and Maria Garcia — are aware something is taking longer than expected and are getting anxious. Priya needs to communicate proactively before the silence becomes a crisis.

### Context loaded
**From Lead Card (`lead_id: LEAD-20250315-PDV`):**
- urgency: 4 — motivated buyers in a tight market
- pre_approved: yes / lender: Austin Capital Mortgage, Raj Patel
- budget_max: $715,000 / property at $549,000 — well within range
- direction: both (selling Bouldin Creek, buying Mueller) — sell-first dependency

**Voice profile:** priya.md
- Warm and calm under pressure. Clients trust her to manage uncertainty without alarming them. She does not project confidence she does not have — she projects competence.

**From `_config/team-standards.md`:**
- Section loaded: Hard moments playbook — Financing delays
- "Get ahead of it with title and the other side before it becomes a crisis. A proactive conversation at day 20 saves a deal that a reactive one at day 28 cannot. Financing delays are communication failures as often as they are financial ones."

### Diana's reasoning
Priya does not know yet whether there is a real problem. That uncertainty is precisely why this message needs to go out now, not at day 28 when options narrow. The playbook is clear: financing delays are communication failures as often as they are financial ones. Carlos and Maria are already anxious — silence makes that worse. A message that says "here is where we are, here is what I am doing, here is what you need to know" restores the feeling of being managed by a professional. It does not manufacture alarm. It does not project false confidence. It gives them the truth of what is known and what Priya is doing about the unknown.

### Output
Hi Carlos and Maria —

Quick update on financing — I want to keep you in the loop even when I do not have a full answer yet.

We submitted the appraisal to Austin Capital four days ago, and I have not received an update since. Raj has been responsive throughout underwriting, so I do not want to assume there is a problem — appraisal review can sit in a queue. But with the financing deadline at day 30, I am not waiting for him to call me.

I am reaching out to Raj today to get a status and find out exactly where we are. I am also giving the listing agent and title company a heads up that we are tracking this closely, so there are no surprises on their end if we need a brief extension to get everything buttoned up.

Here is what you need to know: financing delays are common and usually manageable. What matters is getting ahead of them early — which is what we are doing right now.

I will update you as soon as I hear back from Raj, target end of day.

— Priya

*Tone notes: Calm and competent. Acknowledges uncertainty honestly — does not overclaim ("everything is fine") or alarm ("this could be a problem"). The message is: I am managing this.*
*Recommended send time: Morning, so Priya has time to reach Raj and follow up with an update by end of day.*
*Follow-up trigger: Send update by 5pm regardless of outcome — even "still waiting" is better than silence.*

### Why this meets team standards
Proactive communication at day 20, not day 28 — "hard conversations happen early, when they can still change outcomes." Applies the financing delay playbook: gets ahead of it with title and the other side, does not wait for the lender to surface a problem. The message is honest about what is and is not known — "I do not want to assume there is a problem" is not false confidence, it is accurate. Carlos and Maria feel managed, not abandoned. This draft would fail a generic real estate review because it does not say "everything is on track." It passes Diana's review because it tells the clients the truth and shows them what their agent is doing about it.
